There is a fragment of memory in my head from when I was six years old that I go back to now and then.

I had just started first grade in a new school in Chennai. After losing my mother, I had been yanked out of my school in the countryside and brought to the city. My family was learning how to be happy again, in a new place.

Not liking your school is not an uncommon thing in kids, but for me, I had extra dislike for my new school. I missed my old friends, I missed my mom, and I was an utter misfit in the city school. Every minute I was in my class, I was plotting to get out of there.

Then one fine day, I made it happen.

It was 2p, we had finished lunch, and were going over English lessons. I went up to my teacher and I told her I was not feeling well. My stomach hurt badly and I wanted to go home. I told her I knew how to get to my grandparents flat, and I will go there and rest up until my father came to get me. She knew that my grandparents lived nearby. She gave me permission to go, and I left. I found my way to my grandparents' flat.

Upon arriving there and knocking on the door, I found that my grandparents had gone on a trip, leaving behind just my grandma's sister. My grandma's sister was a punctiliously orthodox Brahmin woman. Let me state outright that I completely disapprove of the discriminatory customs that many Brahmin people including my relatives practiced and continue to practice to this day. I do not approve of what my grandma's sister asked of me that day, and the hidden meaning behind it. I merely state what happened, especially because of what is about to happen.

Grandma's sister asked me to take off all my clothes - I was "impure" in my school clothes having been close to non-Brahmins - wash myself, and then come near her. I did as she said, and as a result was sitting stark naked when I heard a loud rap on the door. It was around 4p or 5p. Grandma's sister opened the door, and my dad burst in looking hot and angry. He rushed into the room where I was sitting with a snack in front of me, dragged me out of the room, out of house, down the staircase, through the foyer where boys my age were playing cricket, and into an autorickshaw parked outside. He looked into my eyes, and sternly said, "Never EVER do that again". Emotions poured through me. I felt chastised. I was suddenly afraid. But more than anything, I felt ashamed. I thought of everyone, including the boys in my grandma's flat that had seen me being dragged out, without a shred of clothing on me.

Many things about this incident and the memory of it elude me. For example: How did my teacher let me leave the school alone? Being new in the city, how did I know the way to my grandma's flat? How did a little girl walking alone escape the notice of the many vile elements in the city? Such is the nature of childhood memories: you wrestle with the fragments you can recall and stitch the hazy snapshots into a cogent narrative with many details missing.

But I tell you this story not just because I find it a fascinating episode of my life, albeit the missing details, but because there is some meaning in it that I feel is worth exploring.

It was the first time in my life that I remember being brave and going after what I wanted with confidence.

Let's fast forward fifteen years.

Many years of schooling and societal engineering had elapsed. I was a graduate student in the University of British Columbia. Over my previous four years of Comp Sci education, I had become increasingly drawn to the field of Distributed Systems. It was a hot subject of the day. I had taken a class from the professor that taught Distributed Systems in the university, and I longed to do my thesis in it. However, when the moment came, I grew afraid. I thought and thought and thought, and I began to feel that the subject was beyond me. Everyone I knew in the Distributed Systems lab was WAY smarter than me. I told myself I would likely not be successful in the field.

So I evaluated my options. Doing my research in Distributed Systems would give me terrific learning, but it also presented a huge chance of failure. Doing research in Software Engineering or Databases, on the other hand, would be more up my alley and guaranteed better success. I ended up choosing the safer path. I did my thesis in Software Engineering. With the benefit of hindsight, however, I have realized that my analysis of my fitness for Distributed Systems research that day was plain wrong. It was an irrational fear that I had allowed to take root in my heart and thereby miss an opportunity to do interesting work under a professor I intensely respected.

Now let's compare the two instances.

The six-year old girl that left her school that day showed an enormous amount of courage. She found what her heart desired, made it her goal, worked up the nerve to ask her teacher to let her out of school, and found her own way to her destination. No one taught her how to do it, or inspired her to do it, she just did. Granted many things could have gone wrong that day. And I am not by any means advocating letting schoolchildren roam freely in the streets - that indeed would be an irresponsible thing to do. But that girl pushed the envelope of possibilities in front of her. She boldly tried, hardly thinking of her chances of failure. Over time the same girl had vastly overestimated her chances of failure and stopped herself from doing things she truly wanted to do. What made the difference?

I have come to believe that as children, most of us are more free spirited than what we become as adults. We run around just to feel the rush of air through our hair, climb trees or jungle gyms, do things most adults would consider insane. We know how to feel alive and how to experiment. It's not that we don't fear the unknown - we are afraid of the dark, e.g. - but we cherish freedom and trying things out for ourselves. Over time, though, society drums that free spirit out of us. We learn to become more afraid of risks. Paradoxically, we learn to become less confident in ourselves. We learn to fear failure. We learn to crave approval and 'not looking bad'. We learn to overthink and opt for the safest path.

Let's stop a minute. While it is only wise to apply our learning and carefully evaluate our desires and actions, let's stop overdoing it. The audacity to do things we really want to do is within us. We were born with it. Let's bring that audacity and free-spiritness and courage back into our lives. Let's not become imprisoned within walls of our fears. Let's relearn the joy of experimenting, of boldly going after things that we desire. For as Theodore Roosevelt said:

 

“It is hard to fail, but it is worse never to have tried to succeed.” ― Theodore Roosevelt
